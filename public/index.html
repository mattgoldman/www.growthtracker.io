Common Growth Tracking Questions

Why does compound growth matter?
Paul Graham explains it best: "When I first meet founders and ask what their growth rate is, sometimes they tell me 'We get about a hundred new customers a month'. That's not a rate. What matters is not the absolute number of new customers, but the ratio of new customers to existing ones.

If you're really getting a constant number of new customers every month, you're in trouble, because that means your growth rate is actually decreasing."
Why is compound growth so powerful?
The beautiful thing about compound growth is that tiniest little changes can have HUGE long-term value. A company that grows at 1% a week will grow 1.7x a year.
Weekly Growth Rate Yearly Growth
1% 1.7x
2% 2.8x
5% 12.6x
7% 33.7x
10% 142x
A company that grows at 5% a week will grow 12.6x in a single year. If you're making $1000 a month and growing at 3% a week, in one year you'll be making $4,650 a month. In 3 years, that number will be $100,602 a month. That's the power of compounding.
What's considered a good growth rate?
This is really a company-specific question that you'll need to ask yourself. Crunch your own numbers and determine where to set the bar. But for a benchmark, Y-Combinator startups shoot for 5-7% a week. On average a more realistic goal would be 3% week over week. If you can only manage 1%, it may be a sign you have some bigger issues to address.

What types of metrics should I measure?
You should be measuring metrics relevant to each stage of your funnel (Acquisition, Activation, Retention, Revenue) and trying to push as many customers through to revenue as possible. That said, the following metrics should not go un-tracked:
  • New users added / User growth rate
  • Cost of customer acquisition
  • Lifetime customer value / payback period
  • Number of active users (you'll need to define 'active' for yourself)
  • Revenue
  • Conversion rate to paid users
  • Churn rate
  • Burn rate
